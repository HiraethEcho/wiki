    Great art must score highly on four measures: emotional impact (visual and visceral); technique (masterful and harmonious); concept (relevant or timeless); and originality (of medium, subject, or treatment). There are many ways each of these criteria can be satisfied, both subjectively and objectively, but the media are open-ended. One can build great architecture; paint on canvas or a wall; sculpt wood, rock, or beach; project an image on a building or the Moon; make an installation; stage a happeningвҖҰ The media extend to literature, music, stage, and film.     Before identifying great art, IвҖҷll start by dividing artists from artisans. An artisan is a skilled craftsman who can produce items of exceptional quality. A painting by an artisan will be a close replica of a person or a landscape; an artisan writer will accurately describe a situation, or perhaps a philosophical idea. But in both cases there will be no attempt to code any further information within the work. The piece may have artistic merit, but this was not the intention of the creator. In the case of art, an emotion or another aspect of humanityвҖҷs condition is encoded deliberately by the artist. A view is expressed on reality and on what it means to be human in this reality. In Van GoghвҖҷs Starry Night paintings we are not seeing a replica of the night sky but an abstract expression that speaks directly to our sense of wonder when we look up on a clear balmy night. In music our spirit can be stirred by either the French horns of SibeliusвҖҷs Karelia Suite bringing to mind the vast open snowy wastes of Finland, or an Aboriginal didgeridoo creating images in our mind of Uluru under the soft then blazing red Australian sunrise. In terms of writing, the classic realist novels of the late nineteenth century such as Moby Dick or Middlemarch communicate so much more about the human spirit than their settings of whaling or an English market town might suggest. And the alliteration, metaphors, similes and onomatopoeia of poetry conjure visions, thoughts, and feelings in ways that even the poet cannot imagine.

    But what makes great art? Any art that does not create any new ideas (such as AI-generated art), can be immediately classed as bad or mediocre. Admittedly, much art is a reworking of previous ideas; but a good artist will always bring new ideas to the work. This may only contribute a small amount to it, but it creates a new perspective. Moreover, in all good art, the audience is stimulated to bring their own feelings and experiences to the work. So, what distinguishes great art is the combination of the artisanвҖҷs technical mastery with the imagination and creativity to create an ever expanding range of intriguing and absorbing ideas, and feelings and thoughts that arise with each new encounter with it.

    The best way to approach this question is to start with how art works in any medium. We tend to think of it as reflecting the world, but as Deleuze and Guatarri argue in A Thousand Plateaus (1980) concerning books, artworks donвҖҷt reflect the world so much as they form a rhizome with it. Their rhizomatic approach imagines a complex matrix of roots that comes from all directions and has no center вҖ“ as compared to an arborescent approach (based on tree-like structures), in which there is a first cause or base вҖ“ something like a trunk from which the branches and foliage break away. As an example, think about Alice in Wonderland. You may tend to imagine it in a Tim Burton kind of way: a lush, colorful landscape, like the art of Henri Rousseau. But when you read the books, the prose is actually threadbare and only gives you the minimal information needed вҖ“ a room, a table, a cake, Alice growing bigger and smaller with each bite вҖ“ as well as a lot of absurd dialogue. All the detail was added as it made its way through history, spreading out with cultural filaments into new ideas and takes. But it remains significant within the matrix it works in. That same dynamic is at work in every other work we consider great. This is why the term вҖҳgreatnessвҖҷ, like the term вҖҳgeniusвҖҷ, is best left to historians. If something an artist creates is designated вҖҳgreatвҖҷ, it means it blew people away in that given moment. That would be flattering in itself; but that effect can pass. If historians are still talking about it a hundred years later, itвҖҷs only if it remains a significant node within the rhizomatic network. In short: artistic greatness is what maintains its position in the general discourse.

    еҪўејҸ еҺҹеӣ дёҺжҠҪиұЎдёӯиҖғиҷ‘зҡ„е·ҘдҪңзҡ„жҖ§иҙЁжңүе…ігҖӮиүәжңҜе“ҒеҸҜд»ҘйҮҮеҸ–зҡ„иЎЁзҺ°еҪўејҸйҰ–е…ҲжҳҜйҖҡиҝҮйў„жөӢе°Ҷе®һдҫӢеҢ–иүәжңҜе“Ғзҡ„зү№е®ҡз»“жһ„е’Ңи®ҫи®ЎжқҘжһ„жҖқзҡ„гҖӮиҝҷз§ҚеҪўејҸиҝҳе…·жңүзү№еҫҒпјҢж¶үеҸҠиүәжңҜзҡ„дёҚеҗҢж•ҙдҪ“й…ҚзҪ®пјҢеҢ…жӢ¬иҝҗеҠЁгҖҒжөҒжҙҫе’ҢйЈҺж јвҖ”вҖ”дҫӢеҰӮзҺ°е®һдё»д№үгҖҒжөӘжј«дё»д№үгҖҒеҸӨе…ёдё»д№үе’ҢзҺ°д»Јдё»д№үгҖӮдјҹеӨ§зҡ„иүәжңҜд№ҹеҸҜд»ҘеҜјиҮҙеүҚжүҖжңӘжңүзҡ„еҪўејҸзҡ„еҪўжҲҗгҖӮ жңҖз»Ҳзҡ„еҺҹеӣ  вҖ”вҖ”иүәжңҜдҪңе“Ғзҡ„ зӣ®зҡ„ вҖ”вҖ”д»ҘеӨҡз§Қж–№ејҸе®һзҺ°вҖ”вҖ”еңЁи®ёеӨҡиҜ•еӣҫи§ЈеҶідјҹеӨ§иүәжңҜжһ„жҲҗзҡ„иҙЎзҢ®дёӯеҫ—еҲ°дәҶжҺўзҙўпјҢеҢ…жӢ¬ж–ҮеҢ–еҸІгҖҒзӨҫдјҡеӯҰгҖҒзҫҺеӯҰгҖҒжү№иҜ„е’Ңзӯ–еұ•гҖӮ

    Opinions range from those supportive or celebratory of represented cultural values and beliefs (e.g. Ken Clark in Civilisation), and those which are critical of them, such as John Berger. The protagonists of such apparently opposing positions then dialogue within an вҖҳaesthetic dimensionвҖҷ (Marcuse) to establish what contingently comes to be regarded as вҖҳgreat artвҖҷ.

    Defining an objective measure of greatness seems a forlorn task. Not only do people not agree on which characteristics are criteria of value, even when they do they can disagree on whether a particular work meets those criteria.
    е®ҡд№үдјҹеӨ§зҡ„е®ўи§ӮиЎЎйҮҸж ҮеҮҶдјјд№ҺжҳҜдёҖйЎ№иү°е·Ёзҡ„д»»еҠЎгҖӮдәәд»¬дёҚд»…дёҚеҗҢж„Ҹе“Әдәӣзү№еҫҒжҳҜд»·еҖјж ҮеҮҶпјҢеҚідҪҝд»–д»¬еҗҢж„ҸпјҢд»–д»¬д№ҹеҸҜиғҪеҜ№зү№е®ҡдҪңе“ҒжҳҜеҗҰз¬ҰеҗҲиҝҷдәӣж ҮеҮҶеӯҳеңЁеҲҶжӯ§гҖӮ

    Some have argued that the value of a work depends on its organic unity, by which they mean the way it combines diverse elements into a coherent whole. However, adopting this view does not settle disagreements as to whether or not unity has been achieved. It also seems to preclude miniatures (in any medium) from being great.
    дёҖдәӣдәәи®ӨдёәпјҢдҪңе“Ғзҡ„д»·еҖјеҸ–еҶідәҺе…¶жңүжңәз»ҹдёҖжҖ§пјҢиҝҷж„Ҹе‘ізқҖе®ғе°ҶдёҚеҗҢзҡ„е…ғзҙ з»„еҗҲжҲҗдёҖдёӘиҝһиҙҜзҡ„ж•ҙдҪ“зҡ„ж–№ејҸгҖӮз„¶иҖҢпјҢйҮҮз”ЁиҝҷдёҖи§ӮзӮ№е№¶дёҚиғҪи§ЈеҶіе…ідәҺжҳҜеҗҰе®һзҺ°з»ҹдёҖзҡ„еҲҶжӯ§гҖӮе®ғдјјд№Һд№ҹжҺ’йҷӨдәҶеҫ®зј©жЁЎеһӢпјҲеңЁд»»дҪ•еӘ’дҪ“дёӯпјүйғҪж— жі•жҲҗдёәдјҹеӨ§зҡ„гҖӮ

    Although no work receives universal approval from all those who experience it, there are common responses to works that people describe as great: it can cause a visceral reaction, such as tears or whoops of joy (though some might say that sentimentalism can do this, so itвҖҷs not evidence of greatness); it inspires a drive to re-experience the work; each experience of the work feels new and reveals new detail, although it may initially be hard to appreciate; it seems (close to) unimprovable; and it is experienced as uplifting in some way. If a work of art induces the вҖҳthis is greatвҖҷ response in a significant number of people вҖ“ enough for it to find an enduring audience вҖ“ then maybe this indicates that it has a kind of objective greatness. I donвҖҷt much care for the symphonies of Mahler, for example, but many people sincerely regard them as great, so maybe I should accept that they are.
